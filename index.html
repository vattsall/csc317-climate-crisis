Global warming

Understanding Global Warming and Climate Change Global warming refers to the long-term increase in Earth’s average surface temperature caused mainly by the buildup of greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide and methane in the atmosphere. This warming contributes to broader climate change — shifts in weather patterns, rising sea levels, melting ice caps, and more frequent extreme weather events. These changes affect ecosystems, economies, and communities around the world, making it one of the most urgent challenges of our time. By learning more and taking action, we can help protect our planet for future generations..

Key Facts

Global Temperature

Avg temps rise ~0.03°F (0.018°C) per year. Small annual changes add up—driving sea-level rise and more extreme weather.

Warmest decade: 2010s Goal: +1.5°C limit

Greenhouse Gases

CO₂, CH₄, and N₂O trap heat like a blanket. Human activities increase their levels, warming the planet.

CO₂ ↑ from energy Methane: agriculture

Extreme Weather

Hotter heatwaves, heavier rain, stronger storms, and drier droughts—impacts vary by region.

Heat risk Flood risk

Causes

Heat & Health

  • More frequent, longer heatwaves.
  • Poor air quality stresses hearts & lungs.
  • High risk: elders, kids, outdoor workers.

Agriculture

  • Methane from livestock & rice paddies.
  • Nitrous oxide from fertilizers and soils.
  • Better feed, manure mgmt, and soil health help.

Deforestation

  • Fewer trees = less CO₂ absorbed.
  • Clearing/burning releases stored carbon.
  • Protect & restore forests to remove CO₂.

Transportation

  • Cars, trucks, ships, and planes burn fossil fuels.
  • Transport is one of the largest CO₂ sources worldwide.
  • Electric vehicles and public transit reduce emissions.

Impacts

Heat & Health

More frequent, longer heatwaves raise risks of heat exhaustion and heatstroke. Hotter days worsen air quality (ozone/smoke), stressing hearts and lungs. Older adults, children, and outdoor workers are most vulnerable.

Water

Rising temperatures lead to more droughts and floods. Melting glaciers reduce freshwater supply while heavy rains cause flooding in other regions.

Biodiversity

Changing climates force plants and animals to migrate or die out. Coral reefs, forests, and Arctic habitats are among the most at risk.

Solutions

Mitigation

  • Switch to clean power (solar, wind).
  • Boost efficiency in buildings & industry.
  • Electrify transport; expand transit.
  • Cut methane (oil & gas, waste, livestock).
  • Protect & restore forests and wetlands.

Adaptation

  • Early-warning systems for heat, floods, storms.
  • Cool roofs, shade trees, urban green spaces.
  • Flood defenses; nature-based buffers.
  • Water conservation, reuse, drought planning.
  • Climate-smart agriculture and fire management.

Policy & Finance

  • Standards & carbon pricing to cut emissions.
  • Incentives for clean tech & retrofits.
  • Climate-risk disclosure for businesses.
  • Invest in transit, grids, and resilience.
  • Climate finance & just transition support.

Milestones & Timeline

  1. 1988 — CO₂ concentration reached ≈352 ppm (parts per million). NASA and NOAA identify this as the year global temperatures began rising more rapidly.
  2. 2015 — CO₂ concentration reached ≈400 ppm. The Paris Agreement was adopted, aiming to limit global warming to 1.5°C–2°C.
  3. 2050 — Projection says CO₂ levels could reach ≈480–500 ppm if emissions continue at current rates. To meet global climate goals, net-zero emissions by 2050 are needed.
